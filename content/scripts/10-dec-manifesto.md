# You're Being Programmed Every Day — Here's How to See It
`dec-manifesto` · decode · target 9:00 · ~135 wpm · THE manifesto

**Production:** opens cold on the MINDOS boot lines — delivered flat, machine-adjacent,
with a soft UI-tone under each line (recovered from the retired boot screen; the boot
lives on as this opening). Then the narrator breaks into full human register at
"Those four lines..." and stays human for the rest.

---

[cold open — measured, near-monotone, one line per breath, faint system tone under each]

MINDOS. [p1] Boot. [p2]
Scanning inputs. [p1] Thousands of messages received today. [p2]
Source: unknown. [p1] Consent: not found. [p2]
Write access: [p1] available. [p4]

[full human voice from here]

Those four lines are not science fiction. They are a fairly dry description of
what happened to you before lunch. [p2]

By the time you finish today, several thousand messages will have passed
through your mind. Ads. Headlines. Captions. Song lyrics. Opinions dressed as
news, news dressed as opinion. The offhand comment from a coworker. The
highlight reels of three hundred acquaintances. [p1] You will remember almost
none of them. [p1] And that is exactly why they work. [p2]

Because here is the mechanism, and it's simpler than anyone wants it to be:
your mind does not have a separate door for important information. It has one
door — repetition. [p1] Whatever comes through often enough gets kept.
Whatever gets kept starts to feel familiar. And whatever feels familiar starts
to feel true. [p2] Psychologists have a name for that last step, but you don't
need the name. You've lived it. Think of a song you hate that you know every
word to. [p1] Nobody sat you down to teach you that song. It just... played.
[p2] Now understand: everything plays like that. Beliefs included. [p2]

So ask the uncomfortable question. [p1] The beliefs running your life right now
— about money, about your body, about what people like you get to do — where
did they come from? [p2] Name one belief you hold that you actually chose.
Sat down, examined the options, and selected. [p2] Take your time. [p2] Most
people can't do it. Not because they're weak — because that's not how beliefs
arrive. They arrive the way the song arrived. Through the open door, on
repeat, while you were busy living. [p2]

And the door has never been busier. [p1] For most of human history, the voices
that could reach you daily could fit in one village. Now the whole world can
bid for your attention — and it does bid, because your attention converts.
Somewhere, the urgency you felt this morning was a design decision. The
inadequacy that flickered when you opened that app — a metric moved. [p1] This
isn't a conspiracy; it's just incentives. Nobody needs to want you anxious.
They only need anxious to hold your gaze two seconds longer. The programming
is a side effect that nobody is responsible for and everybody profits from.
[p2] Source: unknown. Consent: not found. [p2]

Now — here is where this stops being bleak. [p1] Go back to the fourth boot
line. The one that matters. [p2] Write access: available. [p2]

The same door that let all of it in — repetition — is standing open for you,
too. The mechanism has no loyalty. It doesn't care who's doing the repeating.
It will compile whatever plays most often, and for the first time, you can be
the one choosing the rotation. [p2]

This is what mind coding actually is. Not positive thinking — thinking is
upstream of this. Not pretending your problems away. [p1] It is the deliberate,
slightly unglamorous practice of putting chosen inputs on repeat until they
out-repeat the accidental ones. [p1] The words you hear first in the morning.
The sentence you rehearse when it gets hard. The picture you visit of where
this is all going. Chosen. Scheduled. Played again. [p2] One intentional input
might shift a moment. Repeated daily, it starts to build a default. That's not
mysticism — it's the same machinery that taught you the song you hate. Pointed,
for once, somewhere you actually want to go. [p2]

So here is the practice, and it's almost insultingly small. [p1] Once a day,
check in. Name what's actually running — anxious, stuck, lost, whatever's
true. No performance. [p1] Look at the loop underneath it — not to judge it,
just to see it, because a pattern you can see is a pattern that's lost its
main advantage. [p1] And then choose one input on purpose. One song. One
sentence. One visualization. Press play. [p2] That's it. That's the whole
revolution. It fits inside sixty seconds and it compounds like interest. [p2]

You cannot close the door. The messages will keep coming — thousands more
tomorrow, source unknown, consent not found. [p1] But you were never the door.
[p2] You're the one with write access. [p2]

The programming never stops. [p1] The only question — the question this whole
practice hangs on — is whether it happens by accident... [p1] or by choice.
[p2]

Choose. [p2]

[end — one soft system tone, then silence]
