# Stop Asking for Permission
`narr-permission` · narration · abundance · target 5:30 · ~140 wpm, firm, energizing, no shouting — a door being pointed at

---

Somewhere out there, you believe, is a person holding a clipboard. [p1] And on
that clipboard is your name, and next to your name is a box, and one day —
if you're good, if you're patient, if you get one more credential and wait one
more cycle — that person will check the box. Approved. Allowed to begin. [p2]

I need you to hear this cleanly. [p1] There is no clipboard. [p1] There was
never a clipboard. [p2]

Think about where the waiting came from, because it didn't come from nowhere.
School taught you to raise your hand. Work taught you to wait for the review.
Family taught you which dreams were "realistic." Every one of those systems
installed the same quiet subroutine: before you act — check with the desk.
[p1] It was useful when you were nine. It is costing you a fortune now. [p2]

Because look at what the waiting actually does. [p1] The business idea, waiting
for someone to say it's viable. The price, waiting for someone to say you're
worth it. The move, the pitch, the first chapter, the hard conversation — all
of them dressed and ready, standing in a hallway outside an office that does
not exist. [p2] Meanwhile — and you've noticed this, it's why the waiting
stings — people with half your skill are out there doing the thing. Not because
they were chosen. Because they stopped auditioning. [p2]

Now, precision matters here, so let's draw the line carefully. [p1] There are
real permissions in life. Licenses. Consent. Other people's boundaries. This
isn't about bulldozing those — that's not power, that's just noise. [p1] This
is about the counterfeit permissions. The ones nobody actually has authority
to grant, that you keep applying for anyway. Permission to want what you want.
Permission to charge what it's worth. Permission to take yourself seriously
before the results arrive. [p1] No one on this earth holds those papers. Which
means every application you've filed was addressed to an empty chair. [p2]

Here's the recode. [p1] "I no longer wait to be appointed. I appoint myself."
[p2] Say it however you like — but understand what it means in practice. It
means the next time you catch the checking-in reflex — the drafted message
asking someone whether you should, the poll of five friends, the one more
course before you're ready — you pause, and you ask the only question that was
ever yours to answer: [p1] do I choose this? [p2] And if the answer is yes,
you move. Small is fine. Imperfect is fine. Announced to no one is fine. Moving
is the signature. Moving is you, signing your own form. [p2]

One more thing, because it's the part nobody says. [p1] The first few times
you act without permission, it will feel like getting away with something.
Like an alarm should sound. [p1] Let it feel that way. That feeling is not a
warning — it's the old subroutine, noticing it's been unplugged. It fades.
[p1] What replaces it is quieter and better: the plain, steady sense of a
person who was never waiting to begin with. [p2]

The desk is empty. [p1] The hallway is a door. [p1] Go. [p2]
